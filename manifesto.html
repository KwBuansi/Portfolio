Design Manifesto

The design process. From nngroup.com.

Often, products in the tech industry fail because companies don't adequately understand what their users want. Even when users are considered, without a proper understanding of human-centered design, teams risk building solely what users ask for, which is often not what they truly want. Starting a project can be extremely expensive, so an understanding of the design process, which entails adequate preparation, is a mandatory skill for software developers. My Human Computer Interaction class provided a helpful starting point for learning and applying crucial design and needfinding skills, including interviewing, testing, and communication. I found these five aspects of the design process to be the most vital after completing five design sprints:

  • Establish a plan before building
  • Constrain adequately, but be ambitious.
  • Be ready to solve the same problem multiple times.
  • Always have a target audience in mind and consider as many people within it as possible.
  • Be prepared to learn something new.

1. Establish a plan before building
To avoid building a product that fails to understand, teams must thoroughly conduct needfinding before prototyping, which can take various forms depending on the modality. For example, when my team and I started developing a fitness-tracking app, it made the most sense for us to conduct interviews to understand what users required of our app, given that the primary focus of our project was to identify and solve issues that primarily impact college students. Conversely, it made more sense for us to conduct usability testing when developing our kiosk, as we were focusing on improving our website's user experience and already had a base to build on. This is to say nothing of the prior research and persona creation that are crucial to needfinding. Once the team adequately understands the problem at hand, prototyping may begin, but at a low-fidelity level. Before jumping into an expensive prototype that may resemble the final product, paper sketches and Figma prototypes are highly valuable. One recurring theme I noticed throughout my semester in HCI is that it is often difficult to communicate ideas to group members solely through words. When each team member designs their own low-fidelity concept, and the team converges on one specific idea, there is little room for misunderstandings. Understanding that both your team members and customers are in the planning phase is crucial. Truly, this is where the "human" aspect of human-centered design comes in.

Low-fidelity sketches of the Knotty & Board Prototype Homepage

2. Constrain adequately, but be ambitious.
Once the team has converged on a single low-fidelity prototype, it is still possible to remain ambitious and implement new features to a higher-fidelity prototype in the future. Adding impromptu features may be risky, but it may pay off in the long term as long as all team members are aware of said changes, and they remain relevant and reasonable given the project's scope. This can be easy or difficult, depending on the project's modality. For example, during the third design, since I was primarily in charge of our game's code, I was able to easily add impromptu features such as collision, death screens, and sound effects. In this context, communication was less relevant than, for example, in the final design sprint, where we collectively worked on creating our physical prototype. Additionally, in a physical context, it is difficult to add or remove features without damaging the project's structural integrity, so we largely stuck to adding easy-to-implement features to our preexisting design rather than creating brand-new designs, given our limited time.

Final Prototype of the Knotty & Board Kiosk

3. Be ready to solve the same problem multiple times.
The fourth design sprint required us to modify the final product from our first sprint, our revamped designs for the Knotty & Board website. We ultimately agreed to develop a kiosk based on our previous product to improve the Knotty & Board shopping experience, primarily for elderly users. Despite us already having a solid foundation to work off of, being the web pages we designed and in-class feedback for those pages, the fourth design sprint proved to be the most challenging. This was primarily because I was blindsided by the fact that we were required to work on a project we had already completed. I should not have been, given that iteration is a crucial aspect of the design process.

4. Always have a target audience in mind and consider as many people within it as possible.
Rather than a design sprint, a homework assignment illustrated this concept the best throughout the semester. This homework required us to navigate through various sites without using our keyboard. While likely the simplest, this assignment became the most frustrating throughout the semester. Keyboard use was something I had taken for granted-without it, sites took exponentially longer to navigate through, and specific sites without crucial accessibility features became unavigable. The burden poor accessibility has on differently-abled individuals is not merely inconvenient- it harms productivity (the restriction made it take longer to complete assignments for other classes as well). All this to say that accessibility is essential. A website primarily intended for older people that does not consider those with colorblindness, low vision, or low hand mobility is a bad website. After this experience, I found myself subconsciously checking the accessibility practices of other websites I browse throughout the internet.

5. Be prepared to learn something new.
Just as I did not know how to implement best accessibility practices, nor did I know anything about A-Frame or d3.js before my HCI class, software developers should be ready to learn new skills when working on projects. These skills are not all necessarily coding languages. As previously established, needfinding is crucial to the design process. This entails finding interviewees and skillfully soliciting information from them. While simple at first glance, interviewing is a skill worth an entire course in and of itself, to say nothing of actually discovering interviewees suitable for the project's target audience. Both this and working in teams require people skills that may go under the radar of software developers. These people skills should bleed into the actual design of products, as Don Norman highlights in his book, The Design of Everyday Things. Using his philosophy, misunderstandings about how to use a product are the developer's fault. As such, designers must strive to make their products as clear as possible, particularly through the use of affordances.

By learning and practicing these principles, I learned a consistent design process that proved helpful throughout the semester: gathering and soliciting information, prototyping, user testing, and ultimately iterating. However, having a genuine interest in the problem at hand and striving to make valuable contributions to the group effort are the most helpful aspects in the process. With enough enthusiasm, team members will naturally be willing to do what it takes to create strong, endearing products and be prepared to iterate as often as necessary.
